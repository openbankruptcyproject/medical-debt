Medical Debt in Bankruptcy — Empirical Research

Medical debt is among the most common drivers of consumer bankruptcy filings. This research project tracks medical-debt-driven filings, discharge outcomes, the recent CFPB credit-reporting rule changes, and per-district patterns in medical-driven bankruptcy.

The medical-debt-bankruptcy link

Studies have estimated that medical debt is a contributing factor in roughly 30-60% of consumer bankruptcy filings, depending on methodology. The wide range reflects definitional ambiguity:

  • "Medical debt as primary cause" — narrower definition; estimates ~30-40%
  • "Medical debt as significant factor" — broader definition; estimates ~50-60%
  • "Medical event preceded financial distress" — broadest; even higher

Discharge of medical debt in bankruptcy

Medical debt is generally fully dischargeable in Chapter 7 and Chapter 13 as unsecured debt. Unlike student loans or recent tax debt, there are no special rules limiting medical-debt discharge. This makes bankruptcy one of the most effective tools for medical-debt resolution when other options (negotiation, charity care, payment plans) have been exhausted.

The exception is medical debt secured by liens (rare but exists in some states for unpaid hospital bills). Secured medical debt follows standard secured-debt rules in bankruptcy.

Recent CFPB rule changes

In January 2025, the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau finalized a rule prohibiting most medical debt from appearing on consumer credit reports. The rule:

  • Removes most medical debt from credit reports starting March 2025 (rolling implementation)
  • Applies to debts under $500 (already protected) and most larger medical debts
  • Does NOT eliminate the underlying debt — collectors can still pursue collection through other means
  • Reduces but does not eliminate the credit-score motivator for medical-debt bankruptcy filings
